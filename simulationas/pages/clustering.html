Clustering Methods

Unlabeled data still has structure. The question is how you want to define it.

Partition-based methods chase compact groups, hierarchical methods record nested merges, DBSCAN looks for dense regions, and spectral clustering turns similarity into a graph partitioning problem.

Lab 1

Partition-based clustering: K-means and K-medoids

Both methods alternate assignment and representative updates. K-means uses centroids, while K-medoids requires the representative to stay on an actual data point and therefore resists outliers better.

    Lab 2

    Hierarchical clustering and distance measures

    Agglomerative clustering starts with every point alone and merges the closest clusters. Change the linkage rule to see why single, complete, and average linkage produce different hierarchies.

      Lab 3

      Density-based clustering with DBSCAN

      DBSCAN labels core points, expands dense regions, and leaves sparse outliers alone. It is useful when the clusters are irregularly shaped and you do not want to guess k in advance.

        Lab 4

        Spectral clustering

        Convert the points into a similarity graph, compute the graph Laplacian, and split according to the second-smallest eigenvector. This is useful when the geometric shape is not well described by plain centroids.
